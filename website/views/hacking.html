Hacking Basics I: The five stages of cyberattack

Hacking, by definition, is the act of getting a system to do something that its legitimate users (or possibly even its own designers) expect it not to do. At its heart, it's a sophisticated form of con artistry — and like any good con, it requires preparation and attention to detail to pull off well.

A trained hacker executes an attack in a well-established industry-standard five-step pattern. It looks like this:

  1. Reconnaissance. The hacker will learn about the person or organization they're going to attack. They'll study the personalities of a company's employees and try to discover their motivations, desires, and personal weaknesses. The hacker will later use this knowledge to get someone somewhere within the organization to make a computer-related mistake, or possibly look for mistakes that someone has already made that leave a back door open somewhere.
  2. Scanning. The hacker uses probing tools to examine the victim's machines. Of particular interest is the make and model of the equipment, and what software it's running. The hacker will research the equipment to look for esoteric details that its users or administrators might have missed — sometimes, downloading a PDF of a manual from the manufacturer's website can teach the hacker more about a computer or router than the victims themselves know.
  3. Gaining access. The hacker uses the knowledge gained from the previous steps to get a foothold in the victim's machines. "Access" can mean many things; it could just mean reading a private webpage that only the victim is allowed to see, or it could mean running administrator-level commands on any computer on the victim's network. It all depends on what the hacker's trying to do.
  4. Maintaining access. Having gained a foothold, the hacker will often install secret back doors to make it much easier to attack the system again later. This takes a lot of pressure off of the hacker. She might have gotten onto the system in the first place only because of a glitch or stroke of luck, but setting up back doors will allow her to come back at a time and situation of her choosing, putting her in a much better position to steal or corrupt data and get away with it scot-free.
  5. Cleanup. Having completed the infiltration, a good hacker will remove all trace of ever having been there. They'll scrub system logs, delete their own intrusion tools, reactivate any antivirus systems they might have disabled on their way in, and so on. Typically this will result in the victim being completely unable to see how the attack occurred, much less where it came from. A truly skilled hacker will leave the victim unaware that an attack even took place at all.

This formula is so well-established that it's part of the syllabus for professional hacker training and certification courses. Hackers that deviate from this pattern (such as Danny and his cohort of amateurs) often come to regret it.

Hacking Basics II: The S.T.R.I.D.E. threat model

When a hacker gets onto a computer or network, what is she looking to accomplish? The engineers at Microsoft have extensive direct experience with hackers and viruses (which isn't necessarily a point of pride). To keep the different kind of threats straight, they developed a cyberattack classification system called "S.T.R.I.D.E.", revolving around the hacker's goals and motives. The elements of the S.T.R.I.D.E. threat model are as follows:

  • Spoofing: Pretending to be another user (post embarrassing messages from their Twitter, for example).
  • Tampering: Altering data that they're not supposed to be able to touch, such as an account balance.
  • Repudiation: Hide themselves from accountability for their own actions. An example would be a money launderer erasing the bank records of a transfer to his mafia clients, or a burglar erasing the video recordings of his break-in.
  • Information disclosure: Accessing data that the hacker isn't supposed to be allowed to see, such as his boss's emails.
  • Denial of Service (DoS): Preventing a legitimate user from accessing some online resource, such as when Anonymous takes down a website.
  • Elevation of privilege: When a hacker gives herself increased permissions on a system. For example, the hacker could already have a free trial account on an online game, and use her hacking skills to upgrade her account to a paid level.

Many of these elements work in tandem with one another; for example, a hacker can grant herself sysadmin rights (Elevation of privilege) in order to download an otherwise restricted file (Information disclosure). The purpose of such a threat model, ultimately, is to help both engineers and hackers remember what the different possibilities for cyberattack are, so that they can continuously brainstorm about potential weaknesses in a system.

Email tracking

To learn the IP address of Tungsten's corporate headquarters, Danny's team sends Tina an innocuous-looking email rigged with a web bug. The book depicts the tracking image as a large banner across the top of the email, but in practice these are usually completely invisible 1-pixel-wide images called tracking pixels.

There's nothing inherently malicious or harmful in the use of images in an email, even if those images are loaded from a web server. However, it may be worth bearing in mind that, when your browser warns you that there are images in an email and asks if you'd like to view them, the act of clicking "Yes" could potentially inform the sender that the email has been read (and the IP address that it was read at).

Differential release analysis

Using the tracking image in their email, Danny's team learns about the software that Tina is running on her desktop computer. They immediate set to work looking for news and updates for that software, with particular attention to version numbers and release notes.

When hackers find a system running out-of-date programs, they can check the software vendor's website and see what's changed between the old version and the most recent one. Many times, the reason a new version got released is because the old version was found to have some kind of flaw that can expose a security vulnerability. This means that, if hackers know that you're using the old software, they don't need to do any work to find new flaws in it; the flaws are already well-known.

SSHatter vs. SSH and DenyHosts

Every Unix or Linux administrator is surely familiar with SSH, the remote login system that allows a user or admin to execute remote commands and even perform remote desktop operations with the protection of strong encryption. Exploits have been occasionally found in SSH (sometimes based on new cryptographic discoveries fueled by ever-increasing computing power), but they have tended to be very rare, very specific to a particular configuration or situation, and very hard to use in any meaningful way.

There exists a freely available tool called SSHatter that's designed to brute-force its way through an SSH login. As described in Dopamine, SSHatter works by attempting to connect to an SSH server with an astonishingly large number of different login/password combinations, with the hope of randomly stumbling across one that works. The only way to practically use SSHatter is with a botnet, a collection of other people's machines scattered around the Internet, sometimes numbering in the tens of thousands. A hacker typically creates a botnet by writing a virus and spreading it through the web; every machine infected with the virus becomes part of the hacker's botnet, often without the machine's owner knowing it. The hacker can then rent out the botnet for use by his/her hacker friends in executing distributed operations such as SSHatter.

Even with a ten-thousand-node botnet, executing a full SSHatter attack can take days; and, even if the attack runs to completion, success rates tend to be low. However, network administrators who might nonetheless be concerned about such an attack should run an Intrusion Prevention System (IPS) called DenyHosts. It's easy to install and runs almost completely transparently. It protected Tungsten's network from Danny's first hacking attempt, and it will protect yours.

(Incidentally, the tale about anti-sec's breakin of ImageShack via an alleged flaw in OpenSSH is real-world hacker history. It happened in July 2009. Read about how the drama played out from the hype around anti-sec and their manifesto, to the spread of rumors about the OpenSSH flaw, to the ultimate revelation that the entire IT world had just been punked.

Cracking WiFi passwords

When the IEEE 802.11 protocol series — the electronic transmission standard that would come to be popularly known as Wi-Fi — first came out, it didn't come with an encryption scheme; anybody with a properly tuned antenna could read all Wi-Fi transmissions in plain text. The Wi-Fi Alliance temporarily remedied this situation by introducing a cryptographic protocol called Wired Equivalent Privacy (WEP) (a somewhat ironic name in retrospect, but science marches on). Unfortunately, in the early 2000s a series of mathematical analyses of WEP revealed that, by observing a large number of encrypted packets, an eavesdropper could eventually deduce the network password. (The math behind this deduction process is somewhat akin to solving an incredibly large Sudoku.)

Cain and Abel is one of many hacker tools that have been built to exploit WEP's weaknesses. Others include Aircrack-ng and the aptly named WEPCrack. They work by capturing encrypted packets from the air and replaying them back onto the network in order to trick the access point (WiFi hotspot) into transmitting replies; by doing this enough times, they're able to collect a large number of encrypted packets. They then run these encrypted packets through a set of algorithms to find patterns in the encrypted data, which allows them to reconstruct the network password.

Few consumer devices today still run WEP; almost all have switched over to WPA2. However, as was the case with Tungsten's old, discontinued PTZ security cameras, it's not uncommon to encounter networks that still have to support legacy devices, which forces them to operate at the lowest common denominator. These are getting rarer as time goes on, but some say it's only a matter of time before some unassailable flaw is found in WPA in turn.

Proximity cards (or RFID tags)

Tungsten uses standard contactless smart cards to unlock their doors. These are the now-familiar proximity cards that one can carry in one's wallet and open a door merely by wagging one's butt near a keycard reader.

Inside a keycard there's a small microchip, and a long wispy coiled-up antenna. The microchip stores encrypted identifying information, which typically comes down to a serial number (a company that issues these cards to its employees can thus track which employee has which card). However, the card does not contain a battery or other power source, so this microchip is unpowered. That's where the antenna comes in.

The card reader is actually a relatively powerful transmitter. You can think of it like strong spotlight — except, instead of glowing white, it glows at a color corresponding to 13.56 MHz. (RFID-based systems work on the same principles, but at 125 kHz). The keycard's antenna catches the waves being emitted by the reader, and uses their energy to power the microchip. Conceptually, this is similar to using a bright lightbulb to power a solar-powered calculator. Once the microchip has power, it can use that very same antenna to transmit its serial number to the reader.

A well-designed proximity card system will have the reader and the card exchange pre-approved encryption keys (e.g. PGP keys and certificates verifiable through each other's keychains) and use those encryption keys to encode the transmission of the card's number. Proximity card systems that don't take such measures are vulnerable to ProxClone. Quite simply, it's possible (in fact, disturbingly easy) to build an electronic device that radiates EM waves at the same frequency as the proximity card reader, point it at someone's proximity card, and read the card's response; because keycards are responsive even when they're inside someone's wallet, the victim could be on the other end of the room and never know their keycard is being cloned. Later, the hacker transmits that same response back to a real card reader, causing it to think that the person is standing nearby waving their keycard. (See the section on replay attacks, below.)

Naturally, the antenna needs to be very sensitive (and the chip very energy-efficient) to pick up sufficient power to drive the microchip and still have enough milliwatts left over to transmit data back to the reader. These factors contribute to a tiny computing system that, relatively speaking, is rather easy to destroy with a simple power overload — as Danny demonstrated.

Replay attacks

A replay attack is a very common and powerful exploitation technique that takes advantage of an improperly implemented stateless systems. It works by simply watching two system components communicate, recording the conversation, and then going up to one of the components and impersonating the other by playing back its side of the conversation. This can work even against a system that has been built with very strong encryption; the attacker doesn't need to understand the contents of the communications, merely to retransmit them.

Satish performed a replay attack at the packet level, but such vulnerabilities can exist at all levels of hardware/software abstraction. In fact, replay attacks still work at the highest level of abstraction: that of the human being. If you've ever snuck into a VIP party or highly exclusive bar by watching the people who were getting in, eavesdropping on what they were saying to the bouncer, and repeating those same words back to him, then you've already performed a successful replay-attack hack.

There are many ways that an engineer can build a system that will be resistant to replay attacks. One of the quickest and easiest ways to do so is to require one or both sides to transmit a timestamp (ideally along with a hash of the timestamp as well as some other mutually secret piece of data). This way, if a server receives a packet with an old timestamp, then it will know that a replay attack is occurring, thus thwarting the hacker's ability to merely blindly retransmit old traffic.

Locking SIM cards and cellphones

Your phone's SIM card is a standalone microchip that stores codes that can identify you to cellular service providers. The SIM card works closely with your phone's internal electronics, but it's actually its own tiny independent computer. The cellphone cannot access the SIM card's memory directly; it has to request data from the SIM card as with any separate machine.

This means that locking the SIM card is not the same thing as locking the phone. A SIM lock will prevent other people from stealing your SIM card and making calls on it (that you will be billed for), but it will not protect the data on your phone. At best, the phone can remember that it needs to ask the user for a SIM PIN before turning on; however, the phone has no idea what the SIM's correct PIN is. When the user enters a PIN, the phone hands the entered PIN off to the SIM and asks it if it's correct.

The difference between a phone lock and a SIM lock has been known to confuse some consumers, so some phone manufacturers (especially for cheaper, more disposable models) have simplified the locking process by just using a SIM lock for both. The problem with this, of course, is that this makes the phone depend on its SIM card to tell it what its password is. As Danny showed, circumventing a locked phone like this is as easy as swapping the SIM card.

Spoofing emails

When engineers first invented computer networks, email was one of the first applications they thought to create. Various small and easily-built computer-to-computer messaging protocols competed for popularity through the 1960s and '70s. There was no mind paid to digital security at the time. Computers then were large machines taking up the basements of corporate, academic, or military facilities, and pen-and-ink logbooks were used for keeping track of who spent time on them and when. It was a time when engineers were excited that transmitting electronic mail was even possible; a world in which billions of people depended on email for their basic everyday business was still many decades away.

In the early 1980s, the various quick-and-dirty electronic message systems were unified into the Simple Mail Transfer Protocol (SMTP). SMTP was designed to be easy to use and easy to debug — so easy, in fact, that a human being should be able to type text commands to impersonate one side of an email transfer, with the computer on the other end never knowing that it was talking to a human being rather than another machine. This was not a bug; this was a feature.

Fast-forwarding to the modern world, this design decision naturally results in some unforeseen side-effects. Unfortunately, because email was one of the core capabilities for which people bought computers in the first place, SMTP is ubiquitous and thus almost impossible to categorically replace today.

Since the mid-nineties, mail-processing programs have been augmented with various extensions to make up for SMTP's lack of intrinsic security; these include SMTP AUTH, DomainKeys Identified Mail (DKIM), Sender Policy Framework (SPF), and many others. Unfortunately, a mail server has to be configured to actually use these protocols; the setup process is sometimes difficult and frustrating for the network administrator. Moreover, these systems only work when they're communicating with another mailserver that also uses the same system; a chain of email transfers is only as strong as its weakest link.

The last decade has seen an extensive crackdown on insecure mailservers. Ten years ago, barely 10% of mailservers had any kind of authentication; today, that value is over 99%. The ones that don't are called open mail relays; they are of particular interest to spammers because they allow a spammer to send email messages with fake FROM addresses. This is why Mike initially balked at the idea of intentionally setting up an open mail relay; the pernicious power of such a system quickly became clear.

(Note that most real mailservers use a DNS-based blacklist, so that they wouldn't even accept a connection from a known open mail relay; this didn't apply in Mike's case because he took a normally legitimate mail relay owned by his school and set it to operate as an open mail relay temporarily. Whether or not he was ever caught for this, and what impact it had on his grades at Bellevue College, is left to the imagination of the reader.)

Social engineering

Computer hacking, fundamentally, is a form of con artistry. Stripped of all of its technical complexity, hacking involves knowing something that the mark doesn't, and using that gap in the mark's awareness against him. Sometimes this mark is many steps removed from the hacker; it could be a user visiting a website, a sysadmin who built a network, or even an engineer who designed an app. But, more often than you might think, the mark is someone with whom the hacker interacts personally, possibly even face-to-face. Some of the best hackers in history have not been technical wizards or cyber-savants. Rather, they've tended to be people who have learned enough details about how some machine works to merely outsmart a common user; and they've combined that knowledge with the skill of smooth-talking or social finesse to get users to put themselves at risk without even knowing it.

The poster-child of this approach is the legendary Kevin Mitnick, whose career has largely involved tricking people into giving him information that they thought was innocuous but that he could actually use to break into their data systems. Mitnick was a master at acting as though, wherever he was, he belonged there and knew what he was doing — and he generally found that, when acting with sufficient believability, people would cease questioning him and begin instead to question themselves for doubting him. Mitnick showed time and again that the easiest way to get somebody's password is to merely call them up and ask them for it. "Hi, this is Kevin from IT. We're noticing that your computer appears to be running slower than usual. Don't worry, though; we can optimize your system from down here. Can we have your login information, please?"

This works more often than you might imagine. In fact, one of the biggest online scam operations ever documented is occurring at the time of this writing, and it involves random people being called on their phones and told, "We're the Microsoft Windows Support Company, and we've detected viruses on your computer. Please give us remote access so we can remove them." Many people fall for this — and when they do, they find that these scammers aren't there to remove viruses; they're there to install them.

Most of the social engineering in Dopamine is performed by Jason, but Danny finally gets the hang of it when he talks the young, nervous employee at RockBox Security into unwittingly giving Danny full control of his Macintosh workstation. He likewise tries to use disguise and social expectation to grace his way through the Medina Gallante, with a bit less success. Hacking, like any form of conning, is as much art as science.

Reverse shells

When Danny gets a screechy-voiced intern on the phone at RockBox Security, he has the young man type a cryptic command into his Mac's Terminal window. The command, unbeknownst to the intern, had the effect of opening a reverse shell to the kid's console.

A "shell" is a command-line interface that allows a computer's user to control the computer via text. Because it's much easier to send text over a network than to send interactive graphics, shells are the preferred interface among many computer professionals for operating a computer by remote.

Normally, a computer waits for incoming connections, challenges these connections with a username/password prompt, and then permits the connecting user to enter shell commands. This is the normal operating pattern of servers such as SSH, rsh, or telnet.

However, it's possible to make a computer proactively connect to some server on the internet and execute whatever commands the server gives it. This is what happens in a reverse shell. There are many ways to make a computer do this. When Danny tricks the intern into opening a reverse shell through his Mac's OSX command line, Danny first sets up a shell server at Claymore's corporate IP address and then has the kid enter that address and make his Mac invisibly start performing whatever commands Danny types into it from there.

Reverse shells are attractive to hackers because they obviate the need for the hacker to log into the target system. Instead of the hacker breaking in, the reverse shell is essentially an "inside agent" within the victim's machine that "breaks out". Instead of the hacker having to push commands to the target, the target instead goes out to the hacker and asks for commands on its own. Many viruses and malware work this way — not by creating a back door in the sense of a secret user account, but by installing a daemon that runs on the infected machine, periodically reaching out to the hacker's servers and dutifully obeying whatever orders they give it.

Home automation protocols (X10)

Anybody who used the web circa 2002 surely remembers popup ads for X10 home automation kits. X10 comprises a family of gadgets that communicate with one another directly over a house's power lines (radio frequency versions are available as well). An X10 device module can be designed to control an appliance. For example, one can plug a lamp into an X10 device module's socket and the X10 module can turn the lamp on and off, dim it, etc.; an X10 module connected to an air conditioner or heater can regulate a room's temperature. There are even X10 modules for motorized window blinds.

X10 uses bridges to convert X10 signals to IP, so that X10-powered devices can communicate on a computer network. This allows a home rigged with X10 modules to be controlled from a computer, just by clicking on buttons in a software application.

X10 is designed for convenience and capability, not security. X10 modules don't use any kind of passwords or encryption. It probably therefore comes as no surprise that hackers can easily take control of X10 modules simply by plugging a device into a wall outlet that happens to be on the same circuit as the X10 module they want to command. If they find X10 bridges operating on a computer network, then sending fake data to the bridge can result in taking control of all of the X10 devices on that bridge's portion of the building power grid.

There are many home automation protocols, but X10 remains the most popular and well-known one as of this writing. None, however, have particularly great security characteristics, making it possible for a small hacker team like Danny's to easily subvert the automated appliances of an entire high-security building such as the Medina Gallante.

"Rubber-hose cryptanalysis"

What Danny and Eugene do to Ivan's three bodyguards in the sauna is the oldest and most reliable way to gain access to someone's data: put them in a position in which it's "in their best interest" to give it to you.

The industry term "rubber-hose cryptanalysis" (referring to the act of tying someone to a chair and beating them with a rubber hose until they tell you what you want to know) is tongue-in-cheek, but the use of strongarm coercion for someone's data is no laughing matter. Torturing someone to give up their password would not be unheard of in countries like Turkmenistan and other former Soviet republics. Indeed, in Russia there's a common joke about the extremely rapid runtime performance of "thermorectal cryptanalysis", which is the practice of cracking a password by finding the guy who knows the password, shoving a soldering iron up his ass, and turning it on; while it might take a supercomputer millennia to break an encryption scheme, this "algorithm" often produces a result in seconds.

The Western world is only a hair's breadth better. The United States has set legal precedent that a person must disclose their passwords to permit police to look for incriminating files. Meanwhile, in Great Britain, this obligation is codified into law.

Thwarting rubber-hose cryptanalysis involves research into cryptographic deniability. There exist encryption protocols, for example, that can be configured with two different passwords for two different plaintexts. Giving the interrogator one password would unlock some innocuous and uninteresting piece of data, while the "real" password still permits access to the user's real information. These systems are imperfect, however, insofar as an interrogator might still be able to infer that false data has been given; in the case of Danny, Eugene, and the three bodyguards, it would not have been in the bodyguards' interests to give anything but their real information. In almost all cryptosystems, the human user is the most vulnerable point of failure.

(It's worth noting that a team of researchers recently developed a particularly innovative approach in an attempt to prevent password disclosure via coercive interrogation. They came up with a technique to store a password in the motor memory of the user's brain. This makes it impossible for the interrogator to get the user to tell them the password, because the user doesn't consciously know his own password. The technique is far from practical, but the researchers have earned worldwide acclaim for their creativity.)

Man-In-The-Middle (MITM) attacks

When Ivan Zheleznov tries to log onto his Estonian bank account, Satish and Mike are able to redirect his Internet connection to a fake mockup of the bank's real website (presumably built by Moshen, because that is, after all, what he does for a living).

The intercepting of the connection should come as no surprise, considering that Danny's team has pwned the Gallant's routers at that point. Every packet on every computer in the entire hotel has to travel through these routers at some point, so if a router is compromised then all of the traffic going through it is subject to a "Man-In-The-Middle" (MITM) attack.

What this means for most of us is that a hacker doesn't need to plant a virus on your laptop in order to steal your data. After all, sitting between you and the Internet is your cable or DSL modem/router. If a hacker breaks into your cable modem, then she can watch everything you send to the Internet, and every response that the Internet (i.e. your websites, chat sessions, etc.) sends back. In fact, she can even alter the incoming and outgoing packets. A computer sitting behind a compromised router is essentially like a disembodied brain that's been placed into a jar and fed a Matrix-like set of electrical stimuli to fool it into believing it's still interacting with the real world — when in reality, it's completely under the control of an evil mastermind.

Look at your cable or DSL modem/router right now. It's sitting there plugged into your wall, blinking with every packet that it passes between your computer and the Internet. The everyday computer user doesn't realize that this device is a miniature computer in its own right, with its own software and operating system. Your laptop probably has ad blockers, a personal firewall, and at least two different antivirus programs — indeed, you probably get a prompt every other day asking you to update your virus definitions. But what about your router? When's the last time you've updated its virus definition file? Have you ever even logged into your router, much less installed any kind of virus protection on it? You probably should.

Hacking medical implants

Just days before the hacker conference DEF CON 21, the infosec community lost one of its brightest stars. Hacker extraordinaire Barnaby Jack passed away in San Francisco. At the time of this writing, the coroner has not released the cause of death, but has ruled out foul play. He was 36 years old.

Barnaby Jack was a living legend in the hacking world. He caused a sensation at the Black Hat USA 2010 conference in which he demonstrated "Jackpotting", the act of using software to get an ATM to spew out cash like a slot machine.

In 2011, Barnaby Jack turned his attention to an implantable insulin pump produced by medical device firm Medtronic. Because the pump was contained inside the human body, it relied on wireless transmissions from a sensor to determine how much insulin to inject into the patient and when. Barnaby Jack noticed flaws in the communication between the pump and the sensor, and found that he could use those flaws to build a transmitter that would "compromise any vulnerable device within 300 feet." With the ability to trick the pump into dumping its entire supply of insulin at once into the patient's bloodstream, such an attack could be made lethal.

At the DEF CON 21 conference that he never made it to, Barnaby Jack was slated to demonstrate his latest work: wirelessly hacking pacemakers and implanted defibrillators. Modern pacemakers have the ability to transmit diagnostic information and receive calibration instructions — capabilities that provide enormous value to doctors and patients alike. Unfortunately, many of these devices have been built with inadequate security protocols in place, leading to the development of attack techniques that could literally kill a man with a radio transmission.

Some may ask what kind of person would want to figure out how to do these things. The answer is simple: the kind of person who designs and manufactures these devices. If you make a pacemaker with a potentially lethal security flaw that you didn't know about, and it hits the market, then sooner or later somebody will discover that flaw — and heaven help both you and your customers if that someone is, say, Anonymous or the Chinese government. Barnaby Jack's employer, IOActive, is a white-hat security firm that works closely with system manufacturers to fix flaws before they're revealed to the world at large — because revealed they will be, and if not by a white-hat like IOActive, then by... somebody else.
