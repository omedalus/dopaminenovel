Mikhail Voloshin recently left an engineering position at Google to go write analytic software for the finance industry. He attended the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign during the 1990s, where he earned a BS in computer science (with a specialization in machine learning/artificial intelligence) and began a Ph.D. in neurobiology. He left in 2001 for a job with Microsoft's speech recognition team. Once in Seattle, he worked briefly for biotechnology research lab Icos (now CMC Biologics). He soon joined CoCo Communications, a small developer of experimental military communication systems, where he began penning this tale. He now lives in New York City.

He can be reached at dopaminenovel -at- gmail.com.

Projects, presentations, & publications

Brooklyn Brainery: Computer Hacking for Everyone and Rise of the Machines: A History of Computer Technology
Voloshin occasionally teaches at an awesomesauce learning co-op called the Brooklyn Brainery. His teachings include an introductory course on computer hacking and a whirlwind overview of the history of computer technology. Come check it out!
Emerging Methods in Predictive Analytics: Risk Management and Decision-Making
In this industry reference textbook about machine learning applications in the security industry, Voloshin contributes chapters describing algorithms from the world of artificial intelligence and predictive analytics, and shows how they can be applied to network security. In Predictive Network Defense, Voloshin delves into how to use clustering and decision-tree algorithms to improve network firewalls. In Biometric Authentication, he presents a technique for building a statistical model of a user's keyboard typing patterns to determine if a hacker has co-opted a user's console.
AngularJS Meetup: Unit Test Pollution
Engineers use unit tests to find and eliminate software defects before their software goes to production. Here, Voloshin meets with developers of the popular AngularJS front-end web framework, and presents a series of common mistakes that engineers make while building their unit tests, thereby letting potentially dangerous bugs slip into the released product.
UIUC: CyberSlug
Some downright ancient work Voloshin did back in graduate school.
